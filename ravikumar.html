Clay, Water, and Dreams

Ravi Kumar

Terracotta Artist

Every piece of pottery I create on my wheel is a meditation, a prayer, and a piece of my soul shaped by the ancient earth of my homeland.

The first time I touched wet clay on a pottery wheel, I was seven years old. My father, a master potter, placed his large hands over my small ones and together we shaped a simple bowl. I felt the earth breathe beneath my fingers.

Thirty years later, that same magic still captures me every single day. There is something profoundly spiritual about taking a lump of earth and transforming it into something beautiful and useful. It is creation in its purest form.

In our village in West Bengal, pottery is not just a craft—it is our identity. During Durga Puja, our entire community comes alive, creating thousands of idols and diyas. The demand is overwhelming, but so is the joy.

What many do not realize is that behind every terracotta piece is hours of patient work. From preparing the clay to the perfect consistency, to shaping it, drying it, firing it in kilns heated by wood we have carefully selected—each step requires knowledge that has been refined over centuries.

Art, for me, is about staying connected to the earth, to my roots. When someone places one of my pottery pieces in their home, I hope they feel that connection too. I hope they feel the heartbeat of rural India, the dedication of artisan families, and the beauty of slow, intentional creation.
