The Colors of My Grandmother's Hands

Meera Devi

Madhubani Artist

Growing up watching my grandmother create Madhubani paintings taught me that art is not just skill—it is a language of love passed through generations.

I remember sitting on the cool floor of our village home, watching my grandmother's weathered hands move with such grace and purpose. Each stroke of natural color—turmeric yellow, indigo blue, vermillion red—told a story centuries old.

She would say, "Beta, art is not what you paint. It is what you feel when you paint." Those words stayed with me through my journey from a small Bihar village to showcasing my work in galleries across India.

The Madhubani tradition is not just about creating beautiful patterns. It is about preserving our stories, our mythology, our connection to nature. Every peacock, every lotus, every geometric pattern carries meaning that my ancestors embedded in these artworks.

Today, when I teach young girls in my village, I see the same wonder in their eyes that I once had. Art has given me not just a livelihood, but a purpose—to be a bridge between the old world and the new, to keep our traditions alive while making them relevant for today's generation.

The most beautiful part? Every time someone buys a Madhubani painting, they are not just buying art. They are investing in a village woman's dream, supporting her family, and keeping a 3000-year-old tradition breathing.
